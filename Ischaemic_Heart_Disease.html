Ischaemic Heart Disease

Ischaemic Heart Disease (IHD), also known as Coronary Artery Disease (CAD), is a condition characterized by reduced blood flow to the heart muscle due to the build-up of fatty deposits (plaque) in the coronary arteries. This can lead to chest pain (angina), shortness of breath, and other symptoms. In severe cases, it can result in a heart attack. IHD is one of the leading causes of death worldwide, but it is often preventable through lifestyle changes, medication, and in some cases, surgical interventions. Early detection and management are crucial for improving outcomes and quality of life for those affected by this disease.

Further Analysis

In this section, we delve into the intricate details of clinical trial data to provide valuable insights into the evolving landscape of medical research. Through a series of detailed graphs, we explore the changes in single and multi-country trials over the decades, regional trends in clinical trial participation, the average number of countries involved in trials, and the distribution of countries involved per trial. By examining these key metrics, we aim to highlight significant trends, uncover patterns of international collaboration, and identify areas for potential growth and improvement in the conduct of clinical trials worldwide. Now let's navigate through the data to better understand the dynamics and impact of global clinical research efforts.

This graph shows a marked increase in both single-country and multi-country clinical trials starting in the 1990s, with single-country trials peaking at 246 and multi-country trials peaking at 122 in the 2000s. However, both types of trials experienced a decline in the following decades, with single-country trials dropping to 190 in the 2010s and 8 in the 2020s, and multi-country trials decreasing to 62 in the 2010s and 8 in the 2020s. Before the 1990s, the number of trials for both categories was relatively low but showed a steady increase.

The graph shows the trends in clinical trials across different regions from 1980 to 2020. North America had a significant peak in the 2000s with 171 trials, but this dropped sharply to 12 trials in the 2010s and 1 trial in the 2020s. Central Europe also saw a peak in the 2000s with 94 trials, increasing to 100 in the 2010s, and then declining to 5 in the 2020s. Western Europe experienced a peak of 88 trials in the 2000s, falling to 66 in the 2010s and 22 in the 2020s. Asia showed a consistent rise, peaking at 42 trials in the 2000s, decreasing to 24 in the 2010s, and 9 in the 2020s. Other regions, including Latin America, Africa, the Middle East, and Oceania, had lower counts overall, with the highest being Latin America with 68 trials in the 2010s.

The graph on the left shows the average number of countries involved per clinical trial from 1960 to 2020. In the 1960s and 1970s, there were no trials involving multiple countries, as indicated by an average of 0.000. The 1980s saw a slight increase, with an average of 0.048 countries involved per trial. This number rose significantly in the 1990s to 0.455. The highest averages were observed in the 2000s and 2010s, with 2.780 and 2.837 countries involved per trial, respectively. In the 2020s, the average number of countries involved per trial decreased to 0.813.

The graph on the right illustrates the distribution of clinical trials based on the number of countries involved. The majority of trials (847) involve between 0 and 5 countries, represented by the tallest bar. There is a significant drop for trials involving 5-10 countries, with 32 trials. The number of trials continues to decrease as the number of countries increases: 10 trials for 10-15 countries, 15 trials for 15-20 countries, and 4 trials for 20-25 countries. The pattern remains consistent with very low counts for higher ranges, including 3 trials for 25-30 countries, 6 trials for 30-35 countries, and 3 trials for 35-40 countries. The highest ranges, 45-50 and 50-55 countries, have 2 trials each.
